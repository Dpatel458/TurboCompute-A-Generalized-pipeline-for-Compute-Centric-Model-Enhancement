TurboCompute is a model optimization and structural compression platform designed to improve the computational efficiency of deep learning systems while maintaining reliable predictive performance. The platform enables users to upload trained neural network models and apply importance-driven pruning or masking strategies using a configurable global KEEP ratio. The core objective of TurboCompute is to reduce unnecessary computational redundancy in modern neural networks by identifying parameters that contribute minimally to model output and selectively removing or masking them. Deep learning models frequently contain redundant weights that increase floating point operations, memory usage, and inference time without proportionally improving generalization. TurboCompute provides an experimental and educational environment where users can analyze, compress, and evaluate models through structured sparsity techniques aligned with efficiency-focused AI principles.

The platform supports multiple model categories including Feedforward Neural Networks (FNN), Convolutional Neural Networks(CNN), transfer learning architectures such as ResNet, MobileNet and EfficientNet, and transformer-based models. Each model category uses a tailored importance evaluation strategy. For Feedforward Neural Networks (FNN), importance is computed using gradient information, activation behavior, and variance characteristics to capture dynamic contribution of nodes during learning. For Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), importance is computed primarily through weight magnitude analysis, where lower magnitude weights are considered less significant for output stability. For transfer learning architectures, importance is computed using L1 norm-based scoring to measure structural contribution across pretrained layers. Transformer models are currently in development phase and at present support attention head masking, allowing selective reduction in effective attention capacity without fully removing structural components. These importance strategies are internal to the system and are abstracted from the user interface to maintain conceptual clarity without exposing implementation formulas.

TurboCompute applies pruning using a global KEEP ratio. The KEEP ratio determines the percentage of total model parameters retained across the entire network rather than per individual layer. This global strategy ensures that the most important parameters are preserved regardless of their layer location. For example, a KEEP ratio of one hundred percent results in no pruning, while a KEEP ratio of ninety percent retains only the top ninety percent of parameters ranked by importance score and removes or masks the remaining ten percent. This global retention mechanism avoids rigid layer-wise pruning and allows structural flexibility across heterogeneous architectures. In Feedforward Neural Networks and Convolutional Neural Networks, pruning is permanent, meaning low-importance parameters are removed from the model structure. In transfer learning models and transformer architectures, masking is applied instead of permanent removal. Masking reduces effective computation and can stabilize fine-tuning behavior while maintaining structural compatibility with pretrained components.

The primary metric displayed to users after pruning is percentage reduction in floating point operations. FLOPs reduction serves as a direct indicator of computational efficiency improvement. Rather than overwhelming users with multiple complex metrics on the primary interface, TurboCompute emphasizes this computational reduction measure while allowing deeper performance analysis in a dedicated insights section. The insights interface provides comprehensive comparative analytics between the base model and the optimized model. These analytics include overall metric comparison such as parameter count, structural capacity, density levels, and inference speed. Model size comparisons are displayed to highlight storage reduction. Efficiency metrics provide contextual understanding of compression impact. Weight distribution visualization enables inspection of magnitude shifts after pruning. Layer-wise weight magnitude distribution allows users to observe structural concentration of retained parameters. Layer sparsity versus parameter count analysis demonstrates how sparsity is distributed across layers. Cumulative parameter distribution graphs show retention characteristics under varying thresholds. Weight magnitude retention curves illustrate how much structural information is preserved relative to pruning intensity. These visual analytics transform pruning from a black-box action into an interpretable structural experiment.

TurboCompute includes an optional retraining stage that can be applied after pruning. Retraining is intended to recover potential accuracy degradation introduced by parameter reduction. Retraining uses user-provided data and is mandatory only for Feedforward Neural Networks, where dataset upload is required due to architecture flexibility. For Convolutional, transfer learning, and transformer models, retraining operates on user-provided data when available. During retraining, users can configure learning rate and number of training epochs, allowing controlled recovery of performance. Early stopping is implemented to prevent overfitting and unnecessary computation. If instability occurs during retraining, it is recommended to reduce the learning rate by a factor of ten and increase training epochs while applying gradient clipping to stabilize convergence. A learning rate of one multiplied by ten to the power of negative four is generally suitable for most moderate-sized models. Retraining is computationally more expensive and therefore recommended primarily for small to medium models or when moderate KEEP ratios are used.

The architecture visualization interface integrates an external overlay using netron.app. This interface enables users to upload their models and inspect structural composition at a layer-by-layer level. The visualization includes detailed inspection of layer types, parameter structures, and architectural connectivity. The architecture viewer does not modify the model but provides interpretability support. Layer-wise inspection is particularly useful when analyzing pruning sensitivity, as different layers exhibit varying structural robustness. Dense layers, convolutional layers, and attention components may respond differently to pruning intensity. Users are encouraged to perform layer-wise analysis before aggressively reducing KEEP ratios.

TurboCompute is theoretically motivated by concepts related to structured sparsity and generalization behavior. In particular, it draws conceptual inspiration from the phenomenon known as grokking, where models initially achieve high training accuracy but require prolonged training before test accuracy significantly improves. TurboCompute does not claim experimental validation that pruning directly accelerates grokking behavior. Instead, it provides a theoretical perspective suggesting that structured importance-based reduction may reduce redundant learning pathways and encourage earlier stabilization of generalization. By reducing parameter redundancy and constraining model capacity through sparsity, the system hypothesizes that unnecessary prolonged training dynamics may be mitigated. This theoretical framing is presented as academic motivation rather than empirical proof.

From an educational standpoint, TurboCompute functions as a controlled experimentation environment. Students and researchers can observe how structural sparsity affects computational cost, capacity distribution, inference speed, and model robustness. The platform emphasizes interpretability and efficiency rather than black-box compression. It encourages conservative experimentation strategies. Users are advised to begin with high KEEP ratios such as ninety-nine to ninety-nine point nine nine percent and gradually decrease based on validation performance. Validation loss should be monitored closely rather than relying solely on training accuracy. After each pruning cycle, users should compare compression gain against performance drop to identify a stable efficiency-performance tradeoff. The goal is not maximum sparsity but optimal balance.

Different architectures demonstrate different pruning sensitivities. Feedforward networks respond to node-level importance shifts derived from gradient and activation behavior. Convolutional networks respond strongly to magnitude-based filtering. Transfer learning models require cautious masking to avoid destabilizing pretrained representations. Transformer models currently support attention head masking, and further structural pruning is under development. Because structural sensitivity varies, layer-wise inspection through the architecture viewer is recommended before applying aggressive global reductions.

TurboCompute is designed for academic usage and structured experimentation. It provides a semi-technical and semi-academic interface intended primarily for college-level users exploring model compression, structured sparsity, computational efficiency, and theoretical generalization behavior. The platform does not aim to replace industrial-scale compression toolkits but rather to serve as an interpretable and educational framework for controlled experimentation in model pruning and masking.
