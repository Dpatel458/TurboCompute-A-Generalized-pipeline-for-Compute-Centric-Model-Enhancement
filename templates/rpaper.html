Why Masking, Pruning & Grokking Matter for Compute Efficiency

1

Why Dense Models Waste Computation

Neural networks are typically over-parameterized, containing 10-100x more parameters than necessary for the task. Each forward pass multiplies every input through every weight, regardless of whether that connection contributes meaningfully to the output. This means 90% of FLOPs may be spent on noise rather than signal.

2

How Pruning Reduces Effective Training Cost

By identifying and removing low-importance connections before or during training, we reduce the effective network size. A 90% pruned network requires roughly 10x fewer FLOPs per epoch. Combined with the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis, we can find sparse subnetworks that train faster and generalize equally well.

3

Why Grokking Implies Delayed Generalization

The grokking phenomenon reveals that networks often memorize before they understand. Training accuracy rises early while validation stays flat—indicating overfitting. Then, at a critical point, the network "grokks" and validation accuracy jumps. This suggests we're training far longer than necessary for generalization.

4

How Early Sparsity Reduces Total Training Epochs

SNIP and related methods show that connection importance can be determined at initialization. By starting with a sparse architecture, we avoid wasting epochs training connections that will eventually be pruned. This can reduce total training time by 50-80% while maintaining accuracy.

5

Link to Real-World Server Overheating & Energy

Training GPT-3 emitted an estimated 552 metric tons of CO2—equivalent to 120 cars' lifetime emissions. Data centers consume 1-2% of global electricity, with AI workloads growing 300% annually. Pruning and early sparsity directly reduce power draw, cooling requirements, and carbon footprint—making AI development more sustainable.

Core Paper

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis

Jonathan Frankle & Michael Carbin (ICLR 2019)

Abstract

Neural network pruning techniques can reduce the parameter counts of trained networks by over 90%, decreasing storage requirements and improving inference-time performance without sacrificing accuracy. We find that within a large neural network, there exist smaller subnetworks—winning tickets—that can train in isolation to reach comparable accuracy in a comparable number of iterations. These winning tickets are found by training a network, pruning it, and resetting the remaining connections to their initial values.

Key Equations

Pruning mask at iteration $t$:

$$m^{(t)}_{ij} = \mathbb{1}\left[|w^{(t)}_{ij}| \geq \tau^{(t)}\right]$$

where $\tau^{(t)}$ is the threshold for keeping connections at pruning iteration $t$.

Winning ticket reset:

$$\theta^{(0)}_m = m \odot \theta^{(0)}$$

The winning ticket is initialized to the original random initialization, masked by the final pruning mask.

Core Insight

The key finding is that initialization matters. When pruned networks are retrained from scratch with new random initializations, they perform poorly. But when reset to their original initialization (the winning ticket), they match the full network's performance. This suggests that the original initialization, combined with the pruning mask, encodes valuable information about the optimization landscape.

Relevance to This Project

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis validates our approach to masking and pruning: we don't need to train the full dense network to find good sparse architectures. By identifying winning tickets early, we can reduce training compute by 90%+ while maintaining accuracy. This directly addresses our goal of reducing server load and energy consumption.

Single-shot Pruning

SNIP: Single-shot Network Pruning

Namhoon Lee, Thalaiyasingam Ajanthan, & Philip Torr (ICLR 2019)

Abstract

We propose a novel approach that prunes networks at initialization—before any training—based on connection sensitivity. Our method identifies connections that have the largest impact on the loss function at initialization, allowing us to prune up to 95% of connections without ever training the network, while maintaining competitive accuracy after training the sparse network.

Key Equations

Connection sensitivity (importance score):

$$s_{ij} = \left| \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_{ij}} \cdot w_{ij} \right|$$

This measures how much the loss changes when weight $w_{ij}$ is perturbed.

Pruning criterion (keep top-$k$ connections):

$$m_{ij} = \mathbb{1}\left[s_{ij} \geq \tau_k\right]$$

where $\tau_k$ is the threshold that keeps exactly $k$% of connections.

Core Insight

SNIP's breakthrough is that importance can be determined before training. By computing gradients at initialization, we identify which connections would contribute most to learning—without spending compute on training the full network first. This enables "single-shot" pruning: prune once, then train the sparse network directly.

Relevance to This Project

SNIP is foundational to our early-sparsity approach. Instead of training a dense network and then pruning (wasteful), we can apply SNIP at initialization and train sparse from the start. This eliminates the "overhead" phase of dense training, directly reducing total FLOPs and energy consumption by 50-90%.

Magnitude Pruning

Learning both Weights and Connections

Song Han, Jeff Pool, John Tran, & William Dally (NeurIPS 2015)

Abstract

We introduce a three-stage pipeline for neural network compression: (1) train a dense network to learn which connections are important, (2) prune unimportant connections (those with small weights), and (3) retrain the sparse network to fine-tune the remaining connections. Our method reduces the parameter count of AlexNet by 9x and VGG-16 by 13x without loss of accuracy.

Key Equations

Magnitude-based pruning criterion:

$$m_{ij} = \mathbb{1}\left[|w_{ij}| > \theta\right]$$

Connections with magnitude below threshold $\theta$ are removed.

Iterative pruning schedule:

$$\theta^{(t)} = \text{percentile}\left(|\mathbf{W}|, p^{(t)}\right)$$

The threshold increases over iterations to achieve target sparsity $p^{(t)}$.

Core Insight

The magnitude of a weight is a surprisingly effective proxy for importance. Small weights contribute less to the output and can be removed with minimal impact. The key is iterative pruning with retraining: pruning too aggressively at once damages the network, but gradual pruning allows the network to adapt.

Relevance to This Project

Magnitude pruning remains a baseline for comparison. While SNIP prunes at initialization, magnitude pruning provides a strong post-training compression method. Our project can use magnitude pruning as a "second pass" after training to further reduce model size for deployment.

Generalization Phenomenon

Grokking: Generalization Beyond Overfitting

Alethea Power et al. (OpenAI, 2022)

Abstract

We report a surprising phenomenon: small neural networks trained on algorithmic tasks exhibit a phase transition from memorization to generalization. Initially, training accuracy rises while validation accuracy remains near zero—indicating pure memorization. Then, after many additional epochs, validation accuracy suddenly jumps to near-perfect levels. We term this delayed generalization "grokking."

Key Equations

Training dynamics (simplified):

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{train}} \downarrow \text{ rapidly}, \quad \mathcal{L}_{\text{val}} \approx \text{constant}$$ $$\text{After } T_{\text{grok}} \text{ epochs: } \mathcal{L}_{\text{val}} \downarrow \text{ suddenly}$$

Weight decay and grokking:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}} + \lambda \|\mathbf{W}\|_2^2$$

Strong weight decay ($\lambda$) accelerates grokking by favoring simpler solutions.

Core Insight

Grokking reveals that generalization is not gradual—it's a phase transition. The network first memorizes the training data (high capacity, overfitting), then slowly "discards" unnecessary complexity due to regularization, eventually finding a simple, generalizable solution. The delay suggests we're training far longer than necessary.

Relevance to This Project

Grokking has profound implications for compute efficiency. If networks eventually find simple solutions, why train the full complex network for so long? By combining grokking insights with early pruning, we can potentially identify the "generalizable subnetwork" much earlier, stopping training at the grokking point rather than continuing indefinitely.

Learned Sparsity

Learning Sparse Neural Networks via $L_0$ Regularization

Christos Louizos, Max Welling, & Diederik Kingma (ICLR 2018)

Abstract

We propose a method for learning sparse neural networks through gradient-based optimization with $L_0$ regularization. By reparameterizing the $L_0$ norm using hard concrete distributions, we obtain a differentiable relaxation that allows end-to-end training of both weights and sparsity patterns, achieving state-of-the-art compression rates.

Key Equations

$L_0$ regularized objective:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q(z|\pi)}\left[\mathcal{L}(\theta \odot z)\right] + \lambda \sum_i \mathbb{P}(z_i \neq 0)$$

where $z$ are binary gates and $\pi$ are gate parameters.

Hard concrete distribution (reparameterization):

$$u \sim \mathcal{U}(0, 1), \quad s = \text{Sigmoid}\left(\frac{\log u - \log(1-u) + \log \alpha}{\beta}\right)$$ $$\bar{s} = s \cdot (\zeta - \gamma) + \gamma, \quad z = \min(1, \max(0, \bar{s}))$$

Core Insight

The $L_0$ norm (count of non-zero parameters) is non-differentiable, but through stochastic reparameterization with hard concrete distributions, we can optimize it with standard gradient descent. This enables learning sparsity patterns jointly with weights, rather than pruning as a separate step.

Relevance to This Project

Learned sparsity offers a principled alternative to heuristic pruning. By treating sparsity as part of the optimization objective, we can discover compression rates that balance accuracy and efficiency optimally. This complements our SNIP-based initialization approach.

Scaling Laws

Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models

Jared Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, 2020)

Abstract

We study empirical scaling laws for language model performance on the cross-entropy loss. We find that performance depends predictably on model size ($N$), dataset size ($D$), and training compute ($C$) according to power-law relationships. These laws enable predicting the optimal model size for a given compute budget.

Key Equations

Loss vs. model size (power law):

$$L(N) = \left(\frac{N_c}{N}\right)^{\alpha_N}, \quad \alpha_N \approx 0.076$$

As model size increases, loss decreases following a power law.

Loss vs. training compute:

$$L(C) = \left(\frac{C_c}{C}\right)^{\alpha_C}, \quad \alpha_C \approx 0.050$$

Compute-optimal training requires balancing model size and training tokens.

Chinchilla scaling (optimal allocation):

$$N_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{0.5}, \quad D_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{0.5}$$

For a given compute budget, model size and data should scale equally.

Core Insight

Performance scales predictably with resources, but returns diminish. Doubling model size yields only a constant reduction in loss. This means large models are increasingly inefficient—pruning and sparsity can help us achieve similar performance with far less compute by finding the "effective" model size.

Relevance to This Project

Scaling laws tell us that bigger isn't always better. By pruning, we can find smaller models that achieve near-optimal performance for a given task. The power-law relationships help us predict how much we can prune before hitting the accuracy-compute Pareto frontier.

Energy & Policy

Energy and Policy Considerations for Deep Learning

Emma Strubell, Ananya Ganesh, & Andrew McCallum (ACL 2019)

Abstract

We quantify the carbon emissions and financial costs of training modern NLP models. Training a single Transformer model with neural architecture search emits an estimated 284 metric tons of CO2—equivalent to 5 cars' lifetimes. We argue for efficiency as a first-class citizen in model design and provide recommendations for reducing the environmental impact of AI research.

Key Findings

CO2 emissions from training:

$$\text{CO}_2 \text{ (kg)} = \text{Energy (kWh)} \times \text{PUE} \times \text{Carbon Intensity}$$

PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) accounts for data center overhead (~1.58).

Training Dynamics

The Early Phase of Neural Network Training

Jonathan Frankle et al. (ICLR 2020)

Abstract

We study the early phase of neural network training and find that the initial training dynamics critically determine the final performance. The network's trajectory in the first few epochs sets the "course" for the remainder of training. We also find that sparse networks can be trained from the start if the sparsity pattern is chosen carefully.

Key Equations

Linear mode connectivity:

$$\mathcal{L}\left(\gamma \theta_1 + (1-\gamma) \theta_2\right) \approx \gamma \mathcal{L}(\theta_1) + (1-\gamma) \mathcal{L}(\theta_2)$$

If two solutions are linearly connected in loss landscape, they belong to the same basin.

Early training stability:

$$\|\nabla_{\theta} \mathcal{L}(\theta^{(t)})\| \text{ determines long-term trajectory}$$

Gradient norms in early training predict final performance.

Core Insight

The first few epochs of training are disproportionately important. What happens early determines where the network ends up. This means pruning masks identified early (via SNIP or similar) can guide the entire training trajectory, making early sparsity not just possible but potentially beneficial.

Relevance to This Project

This validates our focus on early sparsity. If the early phase matters most, then applying sparsity early (rather than training dense and pruning later) should preserve—or even improve—final performance while dramatically reducing compute. The "linear mode connectivity" finding suggests winning tickets exist in the same loss basin as the full network.
